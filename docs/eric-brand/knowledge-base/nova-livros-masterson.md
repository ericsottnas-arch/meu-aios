# PART 2: BOOK 1 -- "GREAT LEADS: The Six Easiest Ways to Start Any Sales Message"

## Book Details

- **Authors:** Michael Masterson & John Forde
- **Published:** 2012 (AWAI)
- **ISBN:** 978-0982150030
- **Rating:** 4.57/5 (772 ratings on Goodreads), 68% five-star
- **Core thesis:** 80% of the success of an ad or sales letter comes from the lead -- the first 500-800 words

## About Co-Author John Forde

John Forde is a master copywriter who has worked closely with Mark Ford/Michael Masterson at Agora for decades. He is known as one of the most skilled practitioners of lead-writing in the direct response industry.

## The Central Framework: Awareness Spectrum + 6 Lead Types

The book takes Eugene Schwartz's 5 Levels of Customer Awareness (from "Breakthrough Advertising") and makes it PRACTICAL by mapping each awareness level to a specific type of opening (lead) that works best for that audience.

### Eugene Schwartz's 5 Levels of Awareness

| Level | Description | Prospect State |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **Most Aware** | Knows your product, trusts you | "Sup fam!" |
| **Product Aware** | Knows your product exists but hasn't bought | "I know you!" |
| **Solution Aware** | Knows solutions exist but not yours specifically | "I've heard of that." |
| **Problem Aware** | Knows they have a problem but doesn't know solutions | "This sucks!" |
| **Completely Unaware** | Doesn't even know they have a problem | "Life is good yo!" |

### The 6 Lead Types (Direct to Indirect Spectrum)

The leads are ordered from MOST DIRECT (for most aware audiences) to MOST INDIRECT (for completely unaware audiences):

---

#### LEAD 1: THE OFFER LEAD (Most Direct)

**Best for:** Most Aware prospects
**What it is:** A direct appeal that mentions the product, price, discounts, premiums, guarantee upfront
**How it works:** Leads with an emotionally-compelling offer immediately

**Formula:**
1. Lead with the emotionally-compelling offer
2. Underscore the value or benefit
3. Expand on the deal-benefit in following copy
4. Include a compelling "reason why" explaining the offer

**Variation:** The "invitation" lead -- a subtler version that asks prospects to "join" or "try" something

**When to use:** When readers already know, like, and trust you. Existing customers, warm lists, established brand relationships.

**Example principle:** Clear savings (67% discount) + attractive gift = irresistible closer

---

#### LEAD 2: THE PROMISE LEAD

**Best for:** Product Aware prospects
**What it is:** The most common lead type. Centers on the product's biggest benefit/boldest claim
**How it works:** Slightly less direct than offer -- delays mentioning the actual product name but the headline makes the big "promise"

**How to write it:**
- Start with the product's biggest benefit
- Hit the targeted promise right away
- Connect benefits to core desires
- Sound original and new
- Be bold yet believable
- Follow up with strong proof
- Focus on the speed, size, or quality of results

**Historical foundation:** Rooted in 1904 "Reason-Why Advertising" by John E. Kennedy and Albert Lasker -- "you have to give your reader a reason why they should listen to your message"

---

#### LEAD 3: THE PROBLEM-SOLUTION LEAD

**Best for:** Problem Aware / Solution Aware prospects
**What it is:** Identifies the reader's most pressing issue first, then promises an easy, effective resolution
**How it works:** Creates a "hook" showing you understand their pain and have solutions

**Three-step formula:**
1. **Highlight the problem** that keeps the customer up at night (often in the headline)
2. **Empathize** with the reader's emotions, validate the problem, show you understand
3. **Guide them to the solution** -- your offer, product, or service

**The Hook:** What differentiates your offering from everything else in the marketplace

**Template patterns:**
- "If you're feeling [problem], then [solution]"
- "If you have trouble [problem], then try [solution]"

---

#### LEAD 4: THE BIG SECRET LEAD (also called "Velvet Pouch")

**Best for:** Solution Aware prospects
**What it is:** Teases hard-to-come-by knowledge, formula, or "system" throughout the copy
**How it works:** Mesmerizes prospects before revealing the secret. The secret can be a solution, a hidden problem, or a "system" for consistent results.

**Requirements:**
- The secret must be intriguing AND beneficial
- Introduced in the headline
- NOT disclosed in the lead (tease only)
- Progressive clues throughout the letter
- Ordering the product reveals the full secret

**Key principle:** "The longer you keep the prospect mesmerized, the easier the close"

**Best mileage:** When the product itself IS the revelation of the secret

---

#### LEAD 5: THE PROCLAMATION LEAD

**Best for:** Problem Aware / Unaware prospects
**What it is:** Emotionally-compelling declarations that shock, intrigue, or make bold predictions
**How it works:** Suspends disbelief by distracting from the sales pitch. An indirect way to get an unaware reader's attention.

**Rules:**
1. Make the proclamation BOLD
2. Make a promise within it
3. Make the subject relevant to the reader
4. Return to the proclamation at the end

**Examples:** Bold statements, incredible facts, startling predictions, paradigm-shifting declarations

---

#### LEAD 6: THE STORY LEAD (Most Indirect)

**Best for:** Completely Unaware prospects
**What it is:** Uses narrative structure to hook readers emotionally. The most indirect way to open a sales message.
**How it works:** Contains promise implicitly, leans heavily on One Big Idea, includes testimonials, expert biographies, historical proof

**Execution principles:**
- Strong headline telling a story
- Easy-to-remember information
- Convey quantity AND quality
- Carry initial sentiment through to close
- Has a "particularly powerful effect on readers new to you and your product"

**Famous example:** One famous story lead generated $2+ billion in revenue over 30+ years

**Pop culture analogy:** Dos Equis "Most Interesting Man in the World" -- tells the whole story before you even realize it's a beer commercial

---

### THE RULE OF ONE (Core Framework)

The most important principle in the entire book. Every great promotion is anchored by ONE:

| Element | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **One Good Idea** | A clear, central concept | "There's no product easier to create or sell online than a simple e-book" |
| **One Core Emotion** | A single feeling that resonates | "It is simple! I bet I can do it!" |
| **One Captivating Story** | A brief, compelling narrative | "My very first e-book has generated $20,727 in sales" |
| **One Single Desirable Benefit** | A focused outcome readers want | "Make huge profits creating and selling simple e-books" |
| **One Inevitable Response** | A single, clear call-to-action | "Click here now" with limited-time urgency |

**Historical validation:**
- David Ogilvy called this "the Big Idea"
- Victor Schwab: 90 of his Top 100 Headlines were driven by single Big Ideas
- James Loftus: "The more points you try to cover, the less effective each point"

**Key principle:** "One good idea, clearly and convincingly presented, was better than a dozen so-so ideas strung together."

### THE SUPER LEAD (Chapter 9)

A versatile lead type that boosts response across most promotions. It is fun to write and very easy. However, if used for the wrong type of promotion, the sale will fall flat. (The book does not fully disclose this publicly -- it is a key trade secret within the text.)

### Additional Actionable Rules

1. Write in second person always
2. Keep headlines to 7 words maximum
3. Use black headlines (traditional)
4. Provide premium + money-back guarantee
5. Use power words ("free," "now")
6. Include data/statistics proving claims
7. Apply Pareto's Law: 80% of ad impact comes from the first 20% (the lead)
8. Test leads with your specific audience -- effectiveness varies
9. Maintain one core message, even when using different techniques

## Origin Story

The system was developed over nearly a decade, starting in the early 1990s in Florida, refined in 2003 in France, and completed in 2007 in Baltimore.

## The Three Core Frameworks

### FRAMEWORK 1: THE PEER REVIEW

**Created:** 2003 at a copywriting retreat in France
**Refined by:** Mike Palmer, John Forde, and Michael Masterson
**Purpose:** Evaluate and strengthen the headline and lead of a sales letter using group intelligence instead of one person's opinion

**How it works step-by-step:**

1. **Submission:** Copywriter submits a headline and lead to be reviewed separately by a group of peers (4-6 people)
2. **Silent reading:** Group members silently read the copy -- each headline and lead separately
3. **Rating:** Each member rates out loud on a 4-point scale:
   - 4 = 100% certain I would read further
   - 3 = Probably would read further
   - 2 = Probably would not read further
   - 1 = 100% certain I would NOT read further
4. **Threshold check:** If the copy cumulatively scores less than 2.8, the writer is asked to revisit the copy before proceeding
5. **Improvement phase:** If the copy scores 2.8 or higher, any member can suggest specific improvements
6. **Voting:** Remaining members vote on whether each suggestion makes the copy stronger, weaker, or neutral
7. **Writer's choice:** The copywriter decides which suggestions to use when completing the full draft

**Key principle:** No criticism -- only constructive suggestions that get voted on. This prevents ego damage and encourages creative participation.

**Who uses it:** Major publishing houses like Stansberry Research and Agora

---

### FRAMEWORK 2: THE CUB CRITIQUE

**Created:** 2007 in Baltimore
**Purpose:** A super-efficient system for improving everything AFTER the headline and lead (the body, sales argument, and close)

**What CUB stands for:**

| Letter | Means | The Problem |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| **C** | Confusing | You've failed to explain the offer clearly |
| **U** | Unbelievable | You've failed to empathize or provide sufficient proof |
| **B** | Boring | You've failed to engage or maintain interest |

**How it works:**
1. Reviewer reads through the entire body copy
2. Whenever they encounter something confusing, unbelievable, or boring, they **circle that phrase** and mark it with C, U, or B
3. This makes it easy for the writer to scan through and identify exactly what needs changing
4. The writer then addresses each marked section specifically

**Why it works:** These three flaws (confusing, unbelievable, boring) cover the vast majority of all copy failures. If your copy avoids all three, it will almost certainly keep the reader engaged through to the close.

**Application:** Can be done solo or with a reviewer. Fast -- an entire sales letter can be CUB-critiqued in minutes, not hours.

---

### FRAMEWORK 3: THE FOUR-LEGGED STOOL TEST

**Created:** Early 1990s in Florida
**Purpose:** Ensures the four essentials for powerful direct-marketing copy are ALL present. Can be used by copywriters working alone.

**The Four Legs:**

| Leg | Element | What It Means |
|-----|---------|---------------|
| **1** | **One Big, Emotionally Compelling Idea** | A unifying concept that pulls the sales argument together and typically expresses the USP (unique selling proposition) |
| **2** | **Irresistible Benefits Promise** | Clear communication of HOW the product will help the prospect |
| **3** | **Proof for All Claims** | Full track record, evidence, testimonials, data demonstrating the product performs as promised |
| **4** | **Credibility** | Establishing trust for the product, the expert behind it, AND the company offering it |

**The metaphor explained:** A four-legged stool is very stable. Remove one leg -- you can still sit, but uncomfortably. Remove two legs -- you fall over. ALL FOUR legs must be strong and present simultaneously.

**Real-world application:** By using this framework, one client grew their company from zero to more than $10 million in less than three years. They never let a package go out without passing the four-legged stool test.

**Common failure pattern:** The most frequently missing leg is credibility. Companies often have a compelling idea, strong benefits, and decent proof -- but forget to establish WHY the reader should trust THEM specifically.

---

| Stage | Framework | What It Covers |

# PART 4: BOOK 3 -- "THE ARCHITECTURE OF PERSUASION: How to Write Well-Constructed Sales Letters"

## Book Details

- **Author:** Michael Masterson (solo)
- **Published:** 2009/2012 (AWAI)
- **ISBN:** 978-0982150009
- **Rating:** 4.25/5 (166 ratings on Goodreads)
- **Core thesis:** A sales letter is like building a house of persuasion and like courting a romantic partner -- it requires structure, emotional intelligence, and a systematic approach

## The Romance Metaphor

The book uses an extended metaphor of an Indiana Jones-style archaeology professor pursuing a romantic interest. Just as the professor must:
1. Catch someone's eye (attention)
2. Figure out what to say to create interest
3. Build genuine connection and desire
4. Lay groundwork for a long-term relationship

...a copywriter must do the same with a prospect through a sales letter. The metaphor also extends to building a "house of persuasion" that the copywriter and committed customer share.

## The Five-Section Framework (3P Analysis)

Each section is analyzed according to **Purpose, Problem, and Possibilities** -- what each section tries to achieve, what difficulties it must overcome, and how to overcome those obstacles.

### Section 1: THE ENVELOPE TEASER (or Subject Line)

- **Purpose:** Arouse curiosity and create a favorable impression before the letter is even opened
- **Problem:** Getting past the "trash it or open it" decision in seconds
- **Possibilities:** Use intrigue, benefit hints, urgency

### Section 2: THE HEADLINE

- **Purpose:** Capture attention and reflect the initial teaser/promise
- **Problem:** Must be compelling enough to make the reader continue to the lead
- **Possibilities:** Apply the 4 U's (Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-Specific)

### Section 3: THE LEAD (Emotional Copy)

- **Purpose:** Build a powerful EMOTIONAL case for buying
- **Problem:** Tapping into complex emotional needs beyond just "greed and fear"
- **Possibilities:** Use indirect approaches to deepen interest without scaring away the prospect. Call out what is attractive in obtaining desires or avoiding what is unwanted. Use the 6 lead types from "Great Leads"

### Section 4: THE SALES ARGUMENT (Rational Copy)

- **Purpose:** Demonstrate that claims are valid through evidence
- **Problem:** Although emotional appeals are 80% of effectiveness, you still need logical proof
- **Possibilities:**
  - Evidence and data
  - Reasons and explanations
  - Testimonials from users
  - Quotations from authorities
  - Each emotion aroused needs to be supported by several claims
  - Each claim needs several arguments for maximum persuasiveness

### Section 5: THE CLOSE / CALL TO ACTION

- **Purpose:** Drive the specific response desired
- **Problem:** Converting emotional and intellectual buy-in into actual action
- **Possibilities:**
  - The **"False Close"** technique -- make it seem like you are closing, then add more value
  - The **"Liquidating Bonus"** strategy -- add bonuses that make the asking price seem insignificant
  - "There's more" offers (like TV pitchmen)
  - Frame the asking price so it seems low relative to value delivered
  - Together, the false close + liquidating bonus can "boost your sales 100%"

## The 5-Step Persuasion Architecture (Parallel Framework)

The book also presents a parallel AIDA-like framework:

| Step | Action | Purpose |
|------|--------|---------|
| 1 | **Attention** | Capture initial interest |
| 2 | **Interest** | Develop curiosity about the solution |
| 3 | **Desire** | Create emotional AND rational appeal |
| 4 | **Conviction** | Build trust through evidence and logic |
| 5 | **Action** | Drive the specific response desired |

## Key Techniques Taught

1. **Two "little-known power techniques"** to infuse a sales letter with massive power (these may contradict conventional wisdom)
2. **Three critical elements** every successful sales argument must include -- omitting even ONE risks failure
3. **The False Close** -- pretend you're closing, then add surprise value
4. **The Liquidating Bonus** -- stack bonuses so the perceived value dwarfs the price
5. **Emotional + Rational Balance** -- "Complex emotions create deeper connections than simple fear or greed"
6. **Indirect Selling** -- Use storytelling to lower reader resistance rather than hard-sell
7. **Self-Persuasion** -- "The best sales letters don't sell -- they make the reader sell themselves"
8. **Value Hierarchy** -- Build perceived value that significantly exceeds price

# PART 5: BONUS FRAMEWORK -- THE 4 U's (Used Across All Books)

Michael Masterson coined the **4 U's Formula** for evaluating and improving headlines and subject lines:

| U | Meaning | Application |
|---|---------|-------------|
| **Useful** | Does it convey the benefit? | Show what result the reader gets |
| **Urgent** | Does it create time pressure? | Give a reason to act now, not later |
| **Unique** | Is it differentiated? | Show what makes this different from everything else |
| **Ultra-Specific** | Is it detailed? | Use numbers, steps, explicit information, specialized angles |

**How to use:** Rate each headline 1-4 on each U. A headline scoring 3+ on all four U's will almost certainly perform well.

# PART 6: CROSS-BOOK SYNTHESIS -- THE MASTERSON COPYWRITING SYSTEM

When you combine all three books, you get a **complete end-to-end copywriting system:**

```
STEP 1: BEFORE WRITING
├── Identify prospect's awareness level (Great Leads)
├── Select appropriate lead type (Great Leads)
├── Define the One Big Idea (Rule of One)
├── Plan all Four Legs of the stool (Copy Logic)
└── Map the 5-section architecture (Architecture of Persuasion)

STEP 2: WRITE THE HEADLINE
├── Apply the 4 U's (Useful, Urgent, Unique, Ultra-Specific)
├── Keep to 7 words maximum
└── Express the One Big Idea

STEP 3: WRITE THE LEAD (first 500-800 words)
├── Use the appropriate lead type for your audience's awareness
├── Move the prospect EMOTIONALLY first
├── Apply the Rule of One throughout
└── Remember: 80% of success comes from this section

STEP 4: WRITE THE SALES ARGUMENT
├── Support emotional appeals with rational proof
├── Evidence + Reasons + Testimonials + Authority quotes
├── Each claim needs multiple supporting arguments
└── Avoid being Confusing, Unbelievable, or Boring (CUB)

STEP 5: WRITE THE CLOSE
├── Apply the False Close technique
├── Stack Liquidating Bonuses
├── Frame price as small relative to value
├── Drive ONE inevitable response (call-to-action)
└── Return to the One Big Idea from the lead

STEP 6: REVIEW & REFINE
├── Peer Review the headline and lead (group scoring 1-4)
├── CUB Critique the body (mark C, U, or B on problem areas)
├── Four-Legged Stool final check (all 4 legs strong?)
└── Re-check the 4 U's on the headline
```
