## BOOK 1: SCIENTIFIC ADVERTISING by Claude C. Hopkins (1923)

### Also Known As: "A Ciencia da Publicidade" (Portuguese edition - confirmed same book, same author)

---

### AUTHOR BIO: Claude C. Hopkins (1866-1932)

Claude C. Hopkins is considered the father of modern advertising techniques. Born in 1866, he initially studied law before entering advertising. He worked at Bissell Carpet Sweeper Company, Swift & Company, and Dr. Shoop's patent medicine company before being hired in 1907 by Albert Lasker, owner of Lord & Thomas advertising agency, at a salary of $185,000/year (equivalent to approximately $6.4 million in 2025). He finished his career as president and chairman of Lord & Thomas.

**Landmark Campaigns:**
- **Schlitz Beer (1907):** After touring the brewery, Hopkins advertised their meticulous brewing process -- something every beer company did, but none advertised. Sales increased 600%.
- **Pepsodent Toothpaste:** Hopkins turned Pepsodent into a household name. Within a decade, daily tooth brushing jumped from 7% to 65% of the American population.
- **Other famous clients:** Palmolive, six different car brands, numerous products that became market leaders.

He introduced the advertising slogan, popularized test campaigns, and pioneered coupon-based tracking (the origin of modern A/B testing). Published "Scientific Advertising" in 1923 upon retirement, followed by his autobiography "My Life in Advertising" in 1927.

### KEY PRINCIPLES & FRAMEWORKS

**PRINCIPLE 1: Advertising Is Salesmanship**
- "The only purpose of advertising is to make sales. It is profitable or unprofitable according to its actual sales."
- Your ad must function as a salesperson, not entertainment
- Visualize one buyer and ask: would a real salesperson say this face-to-face?
- "Measure ads by salesmen's standards, not by amusement standards."

**PRINCIPLE 2: Test Everything**
- 9 out of 10 ideas fail, making testing essential
- Start with small test campaigns before scaling
- Focus 80% of effort on headlines (good ones multiply returns 5-10x)
- One client reduced customer acquisition costs by 75% over 5 years through 75 different ad iterations
- "Almost any question can be answered, cheaply, quickly and finally, by a test campaign."

**PRINCIPLE 3: Be Specific, Never Vague**
- "A man who makes a specific claim is either telling the truth or a lie. So a definite statement is usually accepted."
- Transform vague boasts: "Fastest shave" becomes "78-second shave"; "World-class" becomes "Used by people in 52 nations"; "Long-lasting" becomes "Lasts 4 hours longer"
- "Platitudes and generalities roll off like water from a duck."
- Specific facts, when stated, have their full weight and effect

**PRINCIPLE 4: Offer Service First**
- "I never ask people to buy. The ads all offer service, perhaps a free sample."
- "Remember the people you address are selfish, as we all are. They care nothing about your interests or profit. They seek service for themselves."
- Lead with value, not with a sales pitch

**PRINCIPLE 5: Tell Your Full Story**
- "The more you tell, the more you sell."
- Once you capture attention, deliver comprehensive information
- Different facts convince different audiences -- omit nothing important
- Long copy outperforms short copy when testing (interested buyers consume pages of information)

**PRINCIPLE 6: Headlines Are Everything**
- Headlines serve to filter and attract only interested prospects
- "The purpose of a headline is to pick out people you can interest. You wish to talk to someone in a crowd. So the first thing you say is, 'Hey there, Bill Jones' to get the right person's attention."
- Quantifiable, specific statements outperform vague claims
- Invest considerable time crafting headlines

**PRINCIPLE 7: Psychology of Advertising (5 Sub-Principles)**
1. Curiosity triggers attention -- unexpected statements capture focus
2. Perceived value, not cheapness -- people judge quality by price; offer bargains, not rock-bottom deals
3. Personalization -- custom touches dramatically increase success
4. Urgency and limits -- "limited time" or "limited quantities" combat procrastination
5. Channel existing desires, don't create them -- tap into trends already moving

**PRINCIPLE 8: Use Samples Strategically**
- "Give samples to interested people only. Give them only to people who exhibit that interest by some effort."
- The word "FREE" generates dramatically more response than discounts
- Reframe the pitch from "buy my product" to "try it free, then decide"

**PRINCIPLE 9: Never Attack Competitors**
- "Attacking a rival is never good advertising."
- Present positive outcomes; showcase results, not problems
- Negative advertising appears selfish and unfair

**PRINCIPLE 10: Individuality & Naming**
- Maintain consistent tone and appearance to build recognition
- Choose names that own a unique word in customers' minds
- Three name formulas: Storytelling names ("Cream of Wheat"), Owner's names (Ford, Kellogg's), Coined names (Vaseline, Kleenex)

### THE 17 FOUNDATIONAL PRINCIPLES OF CONSUMER PSYCHOLOGY

**1. The Fear Factor -- Selling the Scare**
- Fear is most effective when: (a) it scares people, (b) offers a specific recommendation for overcoming the threat, (c) the recommended action is perceived as effective, (d) the recipient believes they can perform the action
- Only ethical when there IS a real problem and your product IS a real solution

**2. Ego Morphing -- Instant Identification**
- Products contribute to a person's ego/self-image
- When you see Victoria's Secret models, it's your ego motivating you to buy
- Most effective for luxury goods, when used by credible individuals, and when it influences how others see the consumer

**3. Transfer -- Credibility by Osmosis**
- Leverage established trusted institutions (logos, endorsements, certifications) to reduce skepticism
- A doctor's white coat, a university seal, an FDA approval stamp

**4. The Bandwagon Effect**
- If a large enough group holds a favorable opinion, that opinion must be correct
- Link your product to groups your target already identifies with
- Three group types: aspirational (want to belong), associative (share values), dissociative (don't want to belong)

**5. The Means-End Chain**
- Don't sell what it does today -- sell what it will do tomorrow
- Focus on future benefits and long-term value rather than immediate results
- Appeal to aspirational outcomes the product enables

**6. The Transtheoretical Model (T-Model)**
- Guide customers through stages: unaware --> familiar --> willing-to-buy
- Avoid "advertising bling" -- don't assume they're ready to buy
- Match message to awareness stage

**7. Inoculation Theory**
- Present a "weak" argument from competitors that tricks the consumer into defending their position, strengthening their attitude toward your product
- Publicize competitors' criticisms and turn them to your advantage

**8. Belief Re-Ranking**
- Shift existing beliefs without triggering defensive reactions
- Support customers' existing ideals while establishing connections to your service

**9. Elaboration Likelihood Model**
- **Central route (high involvement):** Use logical facts, evidence, detailed analysis for expensive/important purchases
- **Peripheral route (low involvement):** Use emotional imagery, visual cues, celebrity endorsements for low-cost items

**10. The 6 Weapons of Influence** (based on Cialdini)
- Comparison/social proof, Liking, Authority, Reciprocation, Commitment/consistency, Scarcity

**11. Message Organization**
- Ensure clarity and easy understanding
- Logical flow of information

**12. Examples vs. Statistics**
- Emotional examples outperform raw data
- Stories beat statistics in persuasion

**13. Message Sidedness -- Dual-Role Persuasion**
- Mentioning drawbacks actually boosts credibility
- "Two-sided" messages are more persuasive than one-sided claims

**14. Repetition and Redundancy**
- Repeat messaging builds familiarity
- Familiarity breeds comfort and trust

**15. Rhetorical Questions**
- Engage conscious consideration
- "Wouldn't you love to look 10 years younger?"

**16. Evidence**
- Present facts showing superior value
- Testimonials, studies, numbers

**17. Heuristics (Length-Implies-Strength)**
- Longer ads with credible facts appear more favorable
- People unconsciously equate thoroughness with credibility

## BOOK 3: HOW TO WRITE A GOOD ADVERTISEMENT by Victor O. Schwab (1962)

---

### AUTHOR BIO: Victor Opper Schwab (1898-1980)

Born March 13, 1898, in Hoboken, New Jersey. At 19, enrolled as a night student at Columbia University where he met Max Sackheim, copy chief for leading mail order agency Ruthrauff & Ryan. Sackheim hired Schwab as his private secretary because he could take shorthand. His contributions to mail order ads, especially for Dale Carnegie's "How to Win Friends and Influence People," helped make it a best seller in the 1930s. This got him promoted to copywriter.

He also wrote copy for the famous Charles Atlas body-building ads and Sherwin Cody's English Course. In 1926, he and partner Robert Beatty bought out Ruthrauff & Ryan and renamed it Schwab and Beatty, where they built the Book-of-the-Month Club into a giant in book subscriptions.

Schwab was a research pioneer -- he used coded coupon ads to split test headlines, appeals, copy length, layouts, and calls to action. Advertising Age called him "the greatest mail-order copywriter of all time."

---

### THE 5 FUNDAMENTALS OF A GOOD ADVERTISEMENT

**Schwab's Master Formula (AIDA precursor):**

**FUNDAMENTAL 1: GET ATTENTION**
- Use a powerful headline as the most reliable method
- Good headlines use specific place names and numbers
- Highly targeted to a specific prospect
- Draw reader into the body copy
- Large, simple photographs of people, children, animals get more attention
- Show product IN USE doing something appealing
- Make ads resemble familiar reading materials, not slick advertisements
- Headlines are TWICE as important as body copy in determining engagement

**FUNDAMENTAL 2: SHOW PEOPLE AN ADVANTAGE**
- Core question every reader asks: "What Will It Do For Me?"
- Schwab's 14 Core Human Desires:
  1. Better health
  2. More money and a better job
  3. Security in old age
  4. Popularity
  5. Praise from others
  6. More comfort
  7. Social advancement
  8. Improved appearance
  9. Personal prestige
  10. More leisure
  11. Pride of accomplishment
  12. Advancement in business/social life
  13. Increased enjoyment
  14. Self-confidence

- Focus first paragraphs on compelling reasons to continue reading
- Rewriting opening lines can substantially boost response rates

**FUNDAMENTAL 3: PROVE IT**
- Most people will NOT believe what you tell them unless you prove it with:
  - Facts and figures
  - Testimonials
  - Scientific proof
  - Demonstrations
  - Endorsements
  - Before/after evidence
- Key insight: While prospects buy for emotional reasons, they NEED logical justification for their purchase
- "An abundance of factual content can enhance sales"

**FUNDAMENTAL 4: PERSUADE PEOPLE TO GRASP THE ADVANTAGE**
- Sum up emotional AND logical reasons to buy
- Help prospects picture themselves already enjoying the benefits (FUTURE PACING)
- "Once your prospect is already imagining himself and the life he'll have when he already has your product, he'll be especially eager to buy."
- Bridge favorable impressions into actual purchase intent

**FUNDAMENTAL 5: ASK FOR ACTION**
- Simply capturing attention is not enough
- Explicitly instruct consumers on what to do next
- Specify exactly what to buy
- Make purchasing simple and clear
- Emphasize immediate action to prevent lost sales
- Create urgency

### HEADLINE SUCCESS PRINCIPLES (from Schwab's analysis)

**Two Principal Attributes of Good Headlines:**
1. They SELECT from total readership those who ARE (or can be induced to be) interested
2. They PROMISE a worthwhile reward for reading

**Specific Patterns That Work:**

| Pattern | Why It Works |
|---------|-------------|
| **"Point of You"** | 43 of 100 headlines use "you/your/yourself" -- people are self-focused |
| **The Specific** | Numbers, amounts, exact figures outperform vague promises |
| **Loss Avoidance** | "People fight much harder to avoid losing than to gain something of greater value" |
| **Curiosity/Questions** | Interrogative headlines that pose questions readers want answered |
| **Before-and-After** | Contrasting negative situations with positive outcomes (#47 "They Laughed...") |
| **Paradox/Underdog** | Contradiction or "fool" ideas resonate emotionally |
| **News Value** | "New" backed by genuine advantages |
| **Prestige Factor** | Promising others will think better of the reader |
| **Negative Approach** | Pinpointing a relatable problem readers recognize in themselves |
| **Natural Language** | Colloquial, unexpected word choices beat stilted ad vocabulary |

| Never be clever at the expense of clarity ("Cleverectomy") | Cashvertising |
| Never use ALL CAPS for long headlines | Cashvertising |

| Never create desire -- channel existing desire | Hopkins |
| Never write for the masses -- write for ONE person | Hopkins |
